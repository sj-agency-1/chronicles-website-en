Chapter 1: Why Business Isn't an Excel Spreadsheet, But a Living Organism with Head Problems.

Has it ever occurred to you that your business is not just a collection of assets, liabilities, and processes? My friends, a business is a living organism: it has its own bloodstream, respiration, organs, body, and, most importantly, its own psychology. It has a head (the owner/TOP management), hands (sales and production), and a nervous system (finance and logistics). And, as in any organism, the crisis doesn't start in the hands and feet—it starts in the head.

The Entrepreneur’s Paradox:

When a business has a "fever" (a crisis), the entrepreneur doesn't look for a psychologist. He looks for a general practitioner who will give him a pill—auditors, analysts, and crisis managers. No sane business person would say, "My business is in crisis, I guess I'll go see a psychoanalyst!"

He seeks money. He looks for an investor or a fundraising specialist. He wastes effort, but the investment won't come, because no one invests in a problematic asset.

He seeks a plan. He hires expensive consultants who provide a "crisis plan that is very complex to implement and requires significant (additional) financing."

The Business Psychologist looks at this frenzy and says: "The cause and beginning of all crises start in the head." Therefore, before paying astronomical sums for external diagnostics, try to address the mental and psychological root causes of the crisis. This is the first stage of Business Psychology. It’s like draining the "abscess of the problem" not surgically, but mentally.

Chapter 2: Who is a Business Psychologist? Not an Empath, But an Anti-Crisis Alchemist.

Let me state immediately: I am not a psychologist. I do not have a specialized psychology degree. I won't be "working through" your childhood traumas. I am an entrepreneur with 28 years of experience and psychology skills. This is a colossal difference.

A standard psychologist, even a brilliant one, cannot effectively help a businessperson specifically with professional operations and, particularly, with a business crisis. Why?

No Business Background: They don't know what LTV, CAC, ROMI, ROAS are, or how to distinguish a good sales funnel from mere busywork.

An entrepreneur will calm down not from meditation, but from an measurable result. They will return to clear thinking only when they receive several viable business tips, start implementing them, and see positive results.

The Value of the Business Psychologist:

My job is not to "muse about a person's general psychological state." My job is to give concrete business advice:

Diagnose the mental block that prevents seeing the obvious.

Transform panic into a clear, financially-sound plan of action.

Chapter 3: Mental "Breaches": When Crisis Hides Behind a Smile.

Businesspeople and TOP managers are used to solving problems "in the kitchen with friends" or in private clubs. This is a charming, but ineffective, form of psychological therapy.

The Irony of Exclusive Clubs: An entrepreneur will never reveal all their secrets to colleagues. And they certainly won't complain about business problems while expecting sound advice. Firstly, it’s shameful: they might be considered ineffective, short-sighted, weak, and even laughed at behind their back. Secondly, it can be dangerous: others might pose a threat to the business by seizing upon some good ideas, striking at weak points, or even organizing a hostile takeover. And overall, this kind of communication ends up being too "diluted" (or "vague"), and the necessary "therapy" is lacking.

Where the Problem Hides and How the Business Psychologist Finds It:

A. The Invisible Ceiling (The "Stagnation in Prosperity" Case)

When a business "hits the ceiling," the entrepreneur tells everyone that everything is great, and that might even be true. But inside, they feel it's stagnation, and behind the outward calm hides a carefully concealed anxiety.

The Business Psychologist's Task: To penetrate this façade of prosperity, uncover the fear of competitors or a future crisis, and provide not motivation, but a new strategy for a breakthrough that will conquer new heights.

B. The Army of Salespeople Revolts (The "Abscess of Abuse" Case)

Sometimes the crisis isn't in the numbers, but in the people. I remember a story when I had to drain the "abscess of wastefulness, abuse, and mere busywork," which was costing the company hundreds of thousands of dollars.

The Business Psychologist's Task: In this situation, I became the one who said, "The emperor has no clothes." I took on the "psychological impact" when an entire army of sales reps came to despise me. But it was precisely thanks to that conflict and a harsh "investigation" that we managed to burn out the bureaucratic fluff and leave only the healthy, working muscles of the business. I helped the owner see personal inefficiency where he only saw "market difficulties."

C. EBITDA in Yogurt (The "Change of Optics" Case)

I was called in to "save" a dairy plant that was incurring losses. Everyone was looking for EBITDA in the milk rivers—that is, in the obvious, large products. But I found it in the yogurt that no one wanted to make, and... in the kvass (a traditional beverage)!

The Business Psychologist's Task: Help the entrepreneur shift their perspective. To move away from the obsessive, obvious "instruction manual" and see an non-obvious, but profitable, alternative. This is an act of pure mental breakthrough: not just "plugging a hole with whatever is handy instead of what's designated," but helping to find that "very" non-obvious, hidden, and unused asset that will "fire" and lift the business to a completely new height.
